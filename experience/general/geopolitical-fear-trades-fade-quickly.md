# Geopolitical Fear Trades Fade Quickly

**Thesis:** Oil and geopolitical-driven market fears often dissipate faster than expected. However, **exiting a position solely because the catalyst fades can be a mistake** — the underlying may have independent drivers (like technical breakdowns) that continue the move even after the catalyst fades.

**Applies to:** general

**Tags:** geopolitical, oil-volatility, fear-trades, momentum, transition, dte-medium

**Confidence:** high

**Last Updated:** 2026-03-20

## Analysis

Geopolitical shocks create immediate market panic that evaporates once headlines shift. However, these shocks often trigger broader regime shifts or structural technical breaks.

**Two-Stage Pattern:**
1. **The Catalyst:** Initial headline (e.g. "Iran strikes") spikes IV and drives price action.
2. **The Trend:** Once the headline shock passes, the stock reverts to its underlying technical reality.

**Key Hindsight Insight (2026-03-20):** 
In the QQQ trades across mid-March, fear of $100 oil faded, but the Nasdaq had independent structural technical weakness. Exiting puts because "fear faded" on March 13 was premature, as the structural trend (below 50 SMA) eventually produced a +43% gain by March 20. Conversely, exiting calls because the "200 SMA broke" saved capital as the trend took over from the catalyst.

**Core Rule:** Don't exit a trend-aligned trade just because the news catalyst faded. A news-driven move that is already aligned with a technical breakdown is a high-conviction "momentum continuation" setup even after the news cools.

## Exceptions & Nuances

- **Independent drivers:** Always check the underlying trend (SMAs) before closing on catalyst fade.
- **Trend-Catalyst Alignment:** If news-driven panic aligns with a technical breakdown, the move is more likely to persist than a purely news-driven spike.
- **Regime Shift:** If the geopolitical event causes a break of the 200-day SMA, the catalyst has triggered a permanent regime shift.
